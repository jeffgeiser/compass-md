# Competitive Strategy

## My view

In early-stage B2B SaaS, competitive strategy is mostly a distraction. Your real competition is inertia and the status quo, not the other vendors. Build something people will switch for, figure out who's willing to switch, and work backward from there.

## Why I hold it

We spent our first year watching competitors. We built comparison matrices. We responded to prospect questions about competitors with carefully worded positioning statements. None of it mattered. The deals we won, we won because we solved a specific problem better than duct tape did. The deals we lost, we lost because the buyer didn't actually have the problem we solved.

The exception is when a competitor changes your market's pricing expectations or when a large platform player enters your space. Both of those require a strategic response. But those are events, not states — you respond when they happen, not by continuously monitoring.

## Where I differ from common takes

Most startup advice says to know your competitive landscape deeply, build comparison matrices, and train sales on "handling objections" about competitors. I think this is mostly wasted energy at our stage. The sales training that actually moves our win rate is about diagnosing whether the prospect has the problem — not about differentiating from Datadog.

The other common take I disagree with: that you need to define a differentiating position and defend it. We don't. We need to be undeniably better for a specific kind of buyer. "Better for X" is a stronger position than "different from Y."

## What would change my mind

If a direct competitor started winning deals we were in. So far that hasn't happened — we lose to "doing nothing" or "building it internally," not to a named competitor. If that changes, I'll revisit.

If we got to a scale where brand and positioning start to matter (e.g., a customer choosing between us and another vendor in a formal RFP where we can't directly demo the product), I'd invest more in explicit competitive positioning.
