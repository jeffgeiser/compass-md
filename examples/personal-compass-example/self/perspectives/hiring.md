# Hiring

## My view

The only hiring mistake that's hard to recover from is hiring someone senior who turns out to be wrong for the company. Junior mistakes are expensive but recoverable. Senior mis-hires change the company's direction in ways that take a year to unwind, if you can unwind them at all.

That means: be slow and specific about senior hires, fast and action-oriented about junior ones. For senior roles, know exactly what you're hiring for — not a job description, but the specific thing that person needs to do in the first 90 days that you can't do without them.

## Why I hold it

The first company I founded had two co-founders (including me). We hired a VP Sales at 12 people based on impressive credentials and a good vibe. She was right for a different kind of company. The unwind took 4 months and cost us two ICs who left in the confusion. We never fully recovered the sales momentum.

At Foundry, I've been slower on every hire above senior IC level and it's been the right call so far. We waited 6 months longer than we "should have" on the sales hire — every month we waited, I got clearer on what we needed. Jordan is a much better fit than the people I was considering 6 months earlier.

## Where I differ from common takes

The conventional wisdom is "hire for potential, not credentials." I partially agree. For IC roles, yes — I'd rather have high potential and low experience than low potential and high experience. For senior roles, I want demonstrated outcomes in situations close enough to ours that I can extrapolate. Potential is harder to evaluate for senior roles, and the cost of being wrong is higher.

I also disagree with "hire fast, fire fast." I think "fire fast" is right — keeping someone around when it's clearly not working is unkind to them and damaging to everyone else. But "hire fast" is only right for junior roles. "Hire right" is better advice for senior ones.

## What would change my mind

If we miss a market window because we were too slow to hire. I hold this view partly because we haven't been under that kind of time pressure. If we were in a winner-take-all race and the choice was between hiring imperfectly fast and losing, I'd update.

## What I got wrong

Early on I thought referral networks were the primary hiring channel. I now think referrals are a good filtering mechanism (low fraud, faster calibration) but not a substitute for a real hiring process. Some of my best hires have come from non-referral channels; I was leaving good candidates on the table by not looking.
