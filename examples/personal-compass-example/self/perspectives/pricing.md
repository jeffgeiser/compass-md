# Pricing

## My view

Price to where you're going, not where you are. If you're selling to enterprise, charge enterprise prices from the start — even when it feels audacious. Underpricing sends a signal about how seriously you take your own product and creates expansion math that's hard to fix later.

## Why I hold it

We launched at $500/month for our first customers. We thought it was a way to reduce friction for early adopters. What it actually did was set a ceiling — every subsequent pricing conversation anchored against that number. It took us 18 months to get to where we should have started.

The customers we brought on at low prices were fine customers, but they weren't the enterprise buyers we're now pursuing. We gave our best early adopters a price that made sense for a different kind of company than we're building. That was a mistake.

The freemium question is related. See `decisions.md` for the full reasoning. Short version: freemium doesn't change the enterprise buying motion; it just adds a tier to maintain. Enterprise buyers don't buy on price; they buy on outcome certainty. Price the outcome.

## Where I differ from common takes

Most early-stage B2B advice says to charge what you can get. I think that's too passive. You should charge what you'll be worth when you're doing this at scale, and then justify the price by actually delivering that outcome now. It concentrates your attention on the right customers.

I also disagree with "charge what you think it's worth, then double it." That's a heuristic, not a strategy. Know your value metrics, know what a solved problem is worth to the buyer, and price proportionally to that. For us: the cost of an undiagnosed incident is $10K-$100K. We charge a small fraction of that and our customers find it easy to justify.

## What would change my mind

If we were losing deals on price in our ICP segment. So far we're not — we're losing on timing ("not a priority this quarter") and on "we'll build it ourselves" (which we've learned to treat as an implementation question, not a price question). If price became the actual blocker, I'd revisit.
