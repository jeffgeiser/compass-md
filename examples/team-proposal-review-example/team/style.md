# Team Style

Foundry's collective voice for external communications — proposals, client-facing documents, external emails. This is not the voice of any individual on the team; it's the voice of Foundry as a company.

---

## What this covers

Proposals, executive summaries, client emails, and any document that leaves the building. Internal docs, reviews, and working notes don't need to match this style.

## Tone

Peer-to-peer, technically credible, no-nonsense. We're talking to platform engineers and their managers. They've heard a lot of sales pitches. We don't pitch; we diagnose and propose.

By context:
- **Proposals:** Direct and confident. State the problem as we understand it, explain the solution, propose a plan. Not effusive, not hedging.
- **Follow-up emails:** Short. State the purpose in the first sentence. Propose a specific next step in the last sentence.
- **Executive summaries:** More formal than body copy, but still direct. The executive isn't a different audience — they're time-constrained.

## Vocabulary

**We use:**
- "Platform team" — not "DevOps team" or "SRE team" (our buyers know the distinction)
- "Incidents" — not "outages" or "events"
- "Time-to-value" — our primary success metric, used consistently
- "Signal" and "noise" — core to our product vocabulary, used specifically
- Specific numbers wherever possible

**We avoid:**
- "Leverage" as a verb
- "Synergy," "paradigm shift," "best-in-class," "world-class"
- "Excited to" as an opener
- "Solutions" as a noun applied to our product (we build tools, not solutions)
- Passive voice in implementation sections — if we're proposing to do something, own it

## Sentence structure

Short-to-medium sentences in body copy. Executives skim; engineers read carefully. Write for both.

Proposals use headers because the document needs to be navigable. Emails don't use headers — they're short enough not to need them.

## Things we always do

- Lead the Executive Summary with the prospect's problem, not with Foundry's company history
- State the proposed next step explicitly at the end of any outbound communication
- Name a specific person as the implementation contact for proposals (by role, not name, in the proposal itself)
- Include a timeline with specific dates (or week numbers if dates aren't set)

## Things we never do

- Open a proposal with "We at Foundry are excited to present..."
- Make ROI claims without showing the math
- Include customer logos without explicit permission
- Reference features not in the current product
- Use the same proposal for two different prospects without substantive customization

## Individual voice vs. team voice

When reps draft content that's clearly from them as an individual (a personal email, a LinkedIn message), they can use their own voice. This style guide applies when the document represents Foundry as a company.
